# Project Euler 985 Solution - Telescoping Triangles

<https://projecteuler.net/problem=985>:

* [985.py](985.py)

This repository contains a simple pure-Python solution for the вҖңTelescoping TrianglesвҖқ problem.

We are given a starting triangle \(T_0\) with integer side lengths. Under certain geometric conditions, we can inscribe a new triangle \(T_{k+1}\) inside \(T_k\). The construction is:

- Each vertex of \(T_{k+1}\) lies on a different side of \(T_k\).
- For each side of \(T_k\), the two sides of \(T_{k+1}\) that meet that side form equal angles with it.

The question asks for the smallest possible perimeter of an integer-sided triangle \(T_0\) such that:

- \(T_{20}\) exists, but
- \(T_{21}\) does **not** exist.

The final numeric answer is printed by `main.py` when executed; it is **not** hard-coded anywhere.

## 1. Geometry and angle recurrence

The key observation is how the **angles** of the triangles transform.

Let the angles of triangle \(T_k\) be \((A_k, B_k, C_k)\) (in radians, summing to \(\pi\)). For the inscribed triangle \(T_{k+1}\) defined by the problemвҖҷs вҖңequal angleвҖқ condition, one can show that:

- Each **side** of \(T_k\) is an **external angle bisector** of the corresponding vertex angle of \(T_{k+1}\).

If the angles of \(T_{k+1}\) are \((\alpha, \beta, \gamma)\), and the angles of \(T_k\) are \((A, B, C)\), then standard angle-bisector geometry leads to the relations

\[
A = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\gamma}{2},\quad
B = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\alpha}{2},\quad
C = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\beta}{2}.
\]

Solving these for \((\alpha, \beta, \gamma)\) in terms of \((A, B, C)\) yields the **forward recurrence**

\[
\alpha = \pi - 2B,\quad
\beta  = \pi - 2C,\quad
\gamma = \pi - 2A.
\]

Thus, if \(T_k\) has angles \((A_k,B_k,C_k)\), then \(T_{k+1}\) has angles

\[
(A_{k+1}, B_{k+1}, C_{k+1}) =
(\pi - 2B_k,\ \pi - 2C_k,\ \pi - 2A_k).
\]

This is exactly the formula implemented in `next_angles`.

### Existence condition

For a triangle to exist, all its angles must be strictly between \(0\) and \(\pi\). From the recurrence, we see that:

- \(T_{k+1}\) exists **iff** all three of \(A_{k+1},B_{k+1},C_{k+1}\) are positive.
- Using the formula above, this is equivalent to \(A_k,B_k,C_k < \frac{\pi}{2}\), i.e. \(T_k\) must be **acute**.

So the process stops when at least one angle at some stage becomes non-acute and the next iteration would yield a non-positive angle.
