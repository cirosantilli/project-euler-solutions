Under RSK, the length of the longest non-strictly increasing subsequence of a
word is the first row of the insertion shape, and the length of the longest
strictly decreasing subsequence is the first column.  Balanced words therefore
have square RSK boundary: first row length equals first column length.  The
alphabet has ten digits, so every relevant shape fits inside a `10 x 10`
square.

For all words over ten digits with a fixed shape `lambda`, RSK gives
`f^lambda * s_lambda(1^10)` words.  The solver evaluates this by the
hook-length and hook-content formulae.

The no-leading-zero condition is handled by reverse-complement symmetry.  A
positive decimal string corresponds to a balanced digit word whose last digit
is not `9`.  Words ending in `9` are easy to subtract: appending `9` increases
the longest non-strictly increasing length by one and leaves the longest
strictly decreasing length unchanged, apart from the one-letter word `9`.
